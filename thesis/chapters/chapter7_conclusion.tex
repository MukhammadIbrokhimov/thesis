\chapter{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{ch:conclusion}

\section{Summary of Findings}
\label{sec:summary}

I compared five machine learning classifiers for predicting diseases from binary symptom vectors: Naive Bayes, Logistic Regression, SVM, Random Forest, and XGBoost. I tested all five on two Kaggle datasets --- one with about 5,000 records and one with about 246,000 --- using identical preprocessing, cross-validation, and evaluation procedures.

Three findings stood out. First, Random Forest and XGBoost consistently beat the other three classifiers across all metrics on both datasets. The margin was clear enough that I would recommend either one over a simpler method for this kind of task. Second, more data helped all classifiers, but the improvement was most noticeable for the ensemble methods and barely visible for Naive Bayes, which hits a performance ceiling due to its independence assumption. Third, most misclassifications happened between diseases that share similar symptoms, which makes sense but is important to keep in mind for any practical application.

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:contributions}

This thesis makes three contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A controlled comparison of five classifiers on the same symptom-based datasets using the same preprocessing and evaluation setup. Most existing studies test one or two classifiers under varying conditions, which makes comparison across papers unreliable.
    \item An evaluation on two datasets of very different sizes, showing how classifier performance scales with data availability. This is something I did not find in the existing literature.
    \item A discussion of what it would take to actually deploy a symptom-based prediction model in a healthcare booking system, including the regulatory requirements under the EU AI Act and GDPR.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Recommendations}
\label{sec:recommendations}

Based on what I found, I recommend the following:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Use XGBoost or Random Forest for symptom-based disease prediction. Both are reliable, and the choice between them can depend on whether you need XGBoost's slightly better performance or Random Forest's simpler setup.
    \item Keep a Logistic Regression model as an interpretable fallback, especially if the system needs to explain its predictions to doctors or regulators.
    \item Use class weighting rather than SMOTE for handling imbalanced classes when the features are binary.
    \item Do not deploy without external validation on real clinical data, confidence thresholds, and human oversight.
\end{itemize}

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future_work}

The most important next step is testing these classifiers on real clinical data. The Kaggle datasets I used here are useful for a controlled comparison, but they do not capture the messiness of actual patient encounters. Partnering with a hospital or clinic to get anonymized records would allow a much more realistic evaluation.

Other directions include adding patient demographics (age, sex, medical history) as additional features, which would likely improve accuracy. Extending the problem from single-disease prediction to multi-label classification would better reflect the reality of comorbidities. And building the actual specialist-mapping layer on top of the disease predictions --- going from ``predicted disease: eczema'' to ``suggested booking: dermatologist'' --- would bridge the gap between the classification task I studied and the booking application that motivated it.

Finally, testing a reduced-feature model that uses only the top 15--20 most important symptoms could show whether a simpler questionnaire achieves similar performance. If it does, that would make the system much more practical for patients who do not want to answer 132 symptom questions.

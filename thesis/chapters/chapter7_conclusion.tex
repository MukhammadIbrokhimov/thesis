\chapter{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{ch:conclusion}

\section{Summary of Findings}
\label{sec:summary}

I used five classifiers --- Naive Bayes, Logistic Regression, SVM, Random Forest, and XGBoost --- with binary symptom vectors to predict diseases. On two Kaggle datasets (one containing around 5,000 instances and the other around 246,000 instances), each classifier was tested with the same preprocessing, cross-validation and evaluation methods. The aim was a controlled comparison to answer two questions: which classifier is most effective for symptom-based disease prediction, and is the answer sensitive to the number of instances in the dataset and the imbalance of classes?

The results support that classifiers have significant differences but no one model worked best for all situations. On Dataset~1, Logistic Regression and SVM outperformed the rest with perfect scores, with Naive Bayes and Random Forest also performing well. On Dataset~2, Naive Bayes had the highest accuracy, while Logistic Regression and XGBoost remained competitive. In general, the optimum model depends on the size of the dataset, the degree of class imbalance, and the balance between accuracy, training cost, and interpretability.

Logistic Regression performed well as a linear baseline. It was not the best on all scores, but it is a nice choice when interpretability is valued more than the last few percentage points of accuracy.

SVM was similar to Logistic Regression in predictive performance but much slower to train, especially on the larger dataset. Its one-vs-one multi-class approach has poor scalability with the number of classes, and is not suitable for this application.

Naive Bayes was a good baseline, and it outperformed the other classifiers on the accuracy measure on Dataset~2. It is also quick to train, which is of great benefit in situations where simplicity and runtime are more important than model complexity.

The extra data changed the ranking, but not uniformly across the board. Some classifiers improved on the larger dataset, while others did not move up. This indicates that the properties of the real data should be taken into account, and the choice of model should not be based on a general assumption that more elaborate techniques always prevail.

In the error analysis, it was found that most of the errors were made between diseases that have similar symptoms. For instance, related respiratory ailments were frequently confused with each other, due to their common symptoms such as cough, fever and chest pain. This is medically acceptable and not necessarily an issue with the healthcare booking use case, since patients with the same pattern of symptoms are typically treated by the same kind of specialist.

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:contributions}

This thesis makes three contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A comparative evaluation of five classifiers on the same symptom-based datasets, with identical preprocessing and evaluation setups. Most current research evaluates only one or two classifiers in different settings, which makes cross-paper comparison unreliable.
    \item A comparison across two datasets of very different size, characterizing the relationship between classifier performance and data availability. I did not find an equivalent comparison in the literature.
    \item A discussion of the steps needed to deploy a symptom-based prediction model in a healthcare booking system, including the regulatory obligations imposed by the EU AI Act and the GDPR.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Recommendations}
\label{sec:recommendations}

Based on the experiments conducted on both datasets and the practical considerations discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion}, I make the following recommendations for anyone considering deploying a symptom-based disease prediction system in a real healthcare booking environment.

\textbf{On model selection.} These results contradict the common conception that more complicated ensemble classifiers are always better than simpler classifiers. On Dataset~2, which is larger and more realistic, Naive Bayes performed best (84.11\%), followed by Logistic Regression (81.82\%), while XGBoost (79.84\%) and especially Random Forest (46.69\%) lagged behind. The choice of model should therefore depend on the characteristics of the dataset used in deployment, namely the number of disease classes, the level of class imbalance, and the degree of symptom overlap --- not by an assumption that the most up-to-date technique will always win.

\textbf{Primary classifier.} For deployment scenarios with many disease classes, real patient-scale volume, and an imbalanced distribution, I recommend Naive Bayes as the primary classifier. It is the most accurate on that data, trains in seconds, generates models small enough to be embedded on the client side, and naturally supports high-dimensional binary feature spaces. Logistic Regression is another safe first option where interpretability is an absolute must, since its coefficients are directly accessible to clinicians and regulators to be inspected and explained.

\textbf{Secondary classifier as a validity check.} I do not recommend using only one model, but rather running a second model as a validity check in parallel with the primary one --- from a different family (e.g., Naive Bayes with Logistic Regression, or Logistic Regression with XGBoost) --- and using their agreement as a confidence indicator. If the two models agree, the prediction can be surfaced to the patient with more confidence. If they don't agree, the system should defer the recommendation to a general practitioner, not a specialist. This is a simple, inexpensive check which does not require well-calibrated probabilities.

\textbf{When to consider XGBoost and Random Forest.} Ensemble methods are still suitable when the dataset is small to medium-sized and the number of classes is moderate (as in Dataset~1), or when the feature set is richer than binary symptoms (for instance, when demographic or severity information is added). They should not be selected by default, and should not be used for datasets containing hundreds of classes and high imbalance without prior empirical evidence that they outperform simpler baselines.

\textbf{On class imbalance.} Class weighting is a better method than synthetic oversampling techniques (e.g., SMOTE) for binary features. Interpolation between binary symptom vectors produces fractional values that do not reflect any realistic patient presentation and can worsen rather than improve performance.

\textbf{On deployment.} A symptom-based disease prediction system should not be deployed without external validation on real clinical data from the target population, calibrated confidence thresholds, and clinical judgement in the loop. The user interface should clearly indicate that the output is a suggestion, not a definitive diagnosis, and patients with severe or deteriorating symptoms should be referred to urgent care regardless of the model's prediction.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future_work}

Although this thesis sets the groundwork for prediction through symptoms, there are still some important areas for future research.

\subsection{Clinical Validation}

The most important future direction is to test these classifiers using real clinical data. The Kaggle datasets employed in these comparisons are helpful for a controlled comparison, but they do not depict the unruly scenes that occur in the real world of patients. Real patients may not report all symptoms, may misunderstand questions, or may describe symptoms differently from the curated training data. The disease distribution of real data in a clinic may also be significantly different from the distribution of the training data.

A collaboration with a hospital or clinic to obtain anonymised patient information would allow a much more realistic evaluation. This would entail gathering symptom data from real patient experiences, reported either via a web form or by a nurse at intake, along with the diagnosis confirmed by a clinician. This real-world data could then be used to train and test classifiers.

Such research would need to be approved by an institutional review board and would need to follow the GDPR and other data-protection laws. It would also have to deal with not having immediate confirmation of diagnosis --- there are some conditions that do not have quick lab tests or imaging to confirm the diagnosis, which may take days or weeks.

\subsection{Incorporating Additional Features}

The datasets I used contain only binary data for symptoms. Adding demographic information (age, sex, medical history) as additional features could enhance prediction accuracy. Age is especially significant because the incidence of disease is different with age: a rash in a child is different from the same rash in an elderly patient.

Medical history is also helpful. A patient with a history of heart disease who complains of chest pain should be handled differently than a healthy 25-year-old reporting the same symptom. However, using medical history brings privacy concerns and complexity to the system.

Another extension is the severity and duration of symptoms. Rather than binary encoding (presence or absence), patients could rate the severity of each symptom on a scale (mild, moderate, severe) and specify how long each symptom has been present. This would provide the classifier with more information, at the cost of a longer and more complex questionnaire.

\subsection{Multi-Label Classification}

Each of my datasets and methods has the assumption that there is one disease per patient. However, in reality, patients may suffer from several conditions at the same time (comorbidities), for instance diabetes and a skin infection, or asthma and seasonal allergies. Multi-label classification would allow the model to predict multiple diseases for a single patient.

This would need training data in which all patients have all their conditions marked, not just the main one. It would also necessitate alterations to the evaluation metrics and to the specialist-mapping logic. When the model indicates both a dermatological and a respiratory condition, who should the patient be referred to? The system might have to prioritize severity of the condition(s) or schedule multiple appointments with specialists.

\subsection{Specialist Mapping Layer}

The disease-to-specialist mapping was not implemented and only the prediction of the disease was done. If this layer were to be built, medical expertise would be needed on which specialists each disease is referred to. Some of the mappings are easy to spot (eczema $\rightarrow$ dermatologist), while others are less straightforward --- a diabetic patient, depending on their main condition (whether it is metabolic or diabetic foot), may require either an endocrinologist or a podiatrist.

The mapping may also be based on the severity of the condition. If you have mild asthma, you can be treated by your GP; if you have severe asthma, you need to be seen by a pulmonologist. The addition of a severity assessment to the specialist recommendation logic is a promising direction for future work.

\subsection{Reduced-Feature Models}

From the feature importance analysis (Section~\ref{sec:feature_importance}), it was seen that certain symptoms are much more informative than others. A reduced-feature model based on only the top 15--20 most important symptoms could be tested to determine whether a simpler questionnaire performs comparably. If so, this would make the system much more practical for patients.

A lengthy yes/no questionnaire (132 questions) is clumsy and can result in poor data quality, as patients can become distracted. A short questionnaire of 15--20 carefully chosen symptoms would be easier to complete and could actually enhance the quality of the data collected, because there would be less accidental or random responding.

The question is which symptoms to retain. The feature-importance rankings are global (averaged over all diseases), so some symptoms could be informative only locally on specific diseases. A careful analysis would be required to choose a subset of symptoms which would provide good coverage across all disease categories.

\subsection{Ensemble Methods and Model Stacking}

Another way is to have multiple classifiers as opposed to selecting one. For example, XGBoost could be used as the primary model, with a Logistic Regression backup: when XGBoost and Logistic Regression disagree, this disagreement indicates uncertainty, and the patient may be referred to a GP instead of a specialist.

In theory, even more powerful ensemble methods (e.g., stacking --- training a meta-model on the outputs of several base models) might be even more effective. However, stacking makes the system more complicated and harder to interpret and maintain.

\subsection{Active Learning and Adaptive Questionnaires}

An adaptive questionnaire can pose follow-up questions depending on the previous answers, rather than asking all 132 questions to all patients. For example, if a patient reports a rash, the system might ask further skin-related questions (itching, location, duration) while skipping irrelevant ones.

This would reduce the length of the questionnaire and make it specific to each patient, but it would also complicate the representation of the features and involve a different modelling approach, such as a decision tree or a reinforcement learning agent.

\subsection{Deployment and Monitoring}

Finally, a full system would be built and deployed with all the practical issues discussed in Section~\ref{sec:deployment} integrated within it: integration with appointment-scheduling systems, calibrated confidence thresholds, handling of edge cases, and ongoing performance monitoring. A pilot deployment in a small clinic or university health centre would provide valuable evidence about what works and what does not work in practice.

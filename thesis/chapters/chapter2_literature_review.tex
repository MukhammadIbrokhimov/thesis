\chapter{Literature Review}
\label{ch:literature_review}

The literature survey conducted in this chapter identifies literature relevant to the machine learning techniques used for disease prediction. It starts with an overview of the use of ML applications in healthcare and then progresses to specific examples. It then goes on to discuss the detail of symptom-based prediction, its problems, and the healthcare booking context that drives this work.

\section{Machine Learning in Health Care}
\label{sec:ml_healthcare}

Machine learning in healthcare has been in practice for more than a decade, but with the digitization of medical records and the advent of low-cost computer power, the field has gotten speedy. \citet{Topol2019} provided a perspective on the status of AI within the healthcare space, suggesting that ML can be as effective as, or more effective than, human clinicians in certain clinical tasks: radiological image interpretation and histopathological slide analysis. The more recent literature has shown that the same type of classifiers achieves very different results in terms of accuracy when applied to different types of data such as laboratory results, patient history, and symptom/ECG measurements~\citep{Mohamed2025}. In this thesis, it is this kind of behaviour I will be studying, comparing five classifiers on two datasets that are very differently scaled and differently structured from a class point of view.

Here, the most frequent application of ML in health care is supervised classification: a model is built from labels on training examples (patient features and diagnosis) and then applied to diagnose new patients. Some of the most popular classifiers are Naive Bayes, Logistic Regression, SVM, Decision Trees, Random Forest, and XGBoost~\citep{Sarker2021}. All five classifiers studied in this thesis are supervised classifiers, but their algorithms belong to different machine-learning paradigms.

According to \citet{Davenport2019}, AI in healthcare can be categorized into three types: clinical decision support, patient engagement, and administrative efficiency. Disease prediction is part of the first type. Most importantly, these models were created to help clinicians and not replace them, and they need to be accurate, understandable, and harmless.

One point that came out of this literature review is that to measure the performance of medical ML on imbalanced datasets, accuracy alone is not enough. On medical data, it can be manipulated to inflate the numbers at the expense of systematically under-diagnosing diseases in minority groups. Therefore, a multi-metric evaluation is used in this thesis (Chapter~\ref{ch:results}).

\section{Symptom-Based Disease Prediction}
\label{sec:symptom_prediction}

Symptom-based disease prediction is one type of supervised classification, where the inputs are patient-reported symptoms and the disease category is the output. It includes basic features as compared to image-based or genomics-based prediction; usually binary, such as ``absent'' or ``present''. This simplicity allows it to be used in low-resource environments and patient-facing applications.

\subsection{Feature Representation Approaches}

Most studies encode symptoms into binary vectors, with each element of the vector corresponding to a specific symptom, and its value being a 1 if the symptom is present, or a 0 if it is not present. It is simple and efficient but does not capture severity or duration. For instance, a mild headache and severe migraine are both represented as \texttt{headache=1}, losing potentially useful information. Although this is a disadvantage, binary vectors are useful for disease classification, especially when ensemble methods are used that can detect feature interactions~\citep{Breiman2001}.

Other representations have been considered. In some studies, ordinal coding is employed, where symptoms are assigned a rating scale (e.g., 0 = not present, 1 = mild, 2 = moderate, 3 = severe). This will involve more specific data collection and will be subjective because the amount of pain one patient may feel is different from another. Even adding complexity doesn't always mean increasing the accuracy of prediction, which is why most studies remain with binary encoding.

The length of a symptom's duration and/or its onset may also be encoded. A symptom that started 2 days ago, for example, might be different from a symptom that began 2 weeks ago. There is little existing research that exploits this temporal information, possibly due to its difficulty of reliable collection and absence from most public datasets.

\subsection{Review of Comparative Studies}

There are some studies that have experimented with a single classifier on symptom data; however, there are very few studies that compare multiple classifiers with the same set of experimental conditions. \citet{Uddin2019} compared Naive Bayes, Decision Trees and Random Forest over several medical datasets and noticed that ensemble methods were better. However, the datasets and preprocessing varied in their experiments, making it difficult to make a definite claim as to which classifier is really best. They also used various train-test splits for classifiers, some balanced and others not.

More surprisingly, \citet{Sarker2021} experimented with several classification algorithms on a diabetes prediction dataset and found Logistic Regression to be quite effective when compared with more advanced approaches. This was argued to be due to the relatively simple decision boundaries of the dataset, which did not necessitate the extra modelling power that is available with ensemble methods. This means that the best classifier will vary based on the type of data, which is one of the reasons why I am testing five different classifiers in this thesis rather than assuming that a single approach will always be victorious.

\citet{Chowdhury2024} performed a systematic literature review on the work of ML-for-diagnosis and found that, although Random Forest and XGBoost are regularly among the top-performing classifiers, very few studies provide comparisons of several classifiers under equal conditions. They specifically requested controlled comparisons with the same preprocessing and evaluation procedures, as this thesis will provide. They reviewed 87 articles published between 2018 and 2023, of which only 12 compared more than three classifiers on the same dataset with the same preprocessing. The other studies generally fine-tuned one or two classifiers and achieved high accuracy, but it is not clear from those results whether other classifiers could have done better.

A recent systematic review by \citet{Alhumaidi2025} reviewed 57 clinical studies using machine learning for real-world data, and found that Random Forest (42\%), Logistic Regression (37\%), and Support Vector Machines (32\%) are the most commonly used classifiers for disease prediction. This supports the model selection in this thesis: tree-based ensembles and linear baselines remain the dominant models in the latest literature, rather than being replaced by more sophisticated deep-learning algorithms.

Another finding from this review is that almost all of the studies include only one data set, making it impossible to determine the generalizability of the findings or whether they apply only to that data. A classifier can have a great test accuracy on a set of 10 disease classes and 50 symptoms but have a bad test accuracy on a set of 40 disease classes and 132 symptoms. Both the number of classes and the number of features can make the classification problem tougher; testing with a single dataset does not provide a complete picture.

Other studies are based on proprietary clinical data from hospitals and are hard to replicate. Open datasets such as those found on Kaggle are easier to access and can be used by other researchers to check results, but they may be less noisy or more uniformly formatted than real clinical data. Therefore, there is a trade-off between reproducibility and realism.

To address this question of generalization, I chose two datasets of vastly different sizes: one containing nearly 5{,}000 records~\citep{Kaggle_Disease2020} and one containing about 246{,}000 records~\citep{Kaggle_Diseases2023}. This enables me to see how the classifiers' ranks change with the size of the data and whether the larger set of data benefits all classifiers or just some of them.

\section{Challenges in Medical Prediction}
\label{sec:challenges}

Medical classification is a subject that is different from the standard ML problems (such as image or text classification). The three major obstacles identified in the literature, which are discussed in this section, are class imbalance, noisy and incomplete symptom data, and the difficulty of evaluation.

\subsection{Class Imbalance}

Class imbalance is the greatest challenge. Some diseases (such as the flu or common cold) are common, while others (such as some autoimmune disorders) are rare, so the training data is biased. One of the easiest ways a classifier could go is to detect the common diseases and to ignore the rare ones completely, even if high overall accuracy can be achieved with it~\citep{Haixiang2017}. For example, if 80\% of patients in a dataset have one of five common diseases, a classifier that always predicts one of those five diseases would achieve 80\% accuracy while being useless on the remaining 36 diseases.

This is why it is misleading to use accuracy on imbalanced data. A classifier achieving a 95\% accuracy rate may be performing well on every class, or it may just be doing well on the common ones while ignoring half of them. For this reason, along with accuracy (see Section~\ref{sec:evaluation_metrics}), I report precision, recall and F1-score.

There are multiple techniques that have been suggested for dealing with class imbalance. The SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique) method, proposed by \citet{Chawla2002}, produces synthetic examples of infrequent classes by interpolating between existing classes. Given two patients with similar symptom patterns from a rare disease, SMOTE constructs a new synthetic patient whose symptom vector is between their two. This boosts the number of training examples for rare diseases and enables classifiers to learn more accurate decision boundaries.

However, as mentioned by \citet{Fernandez2018}, SMOTE is not effective in binary feature spaces because taking the average of binary symptom vectors yields fractional values which do not correspond to a valid presentation of symptoms. For example, if patient A has symptoms $[1, 0, 1, 0]$ and patient B has $[0, 1, 1, 0]$, the interpolated vector might be $[0.5, 0.5, 1, 0]$. There is no meaning in a value of 0.5---whether a symptom is present or not is a binary value. Rounding the interpolated vector to 0 or 1 may produce a set of symptoms that cannot be explained by medical problems.

Another approach is class weighting: the loss is over-weighted when predicting rare diseases. If one disease occurs 100 times in the training set and another only 10 times, the rare disease can be weighted 10 times more heavily, such that a single misclassification of it costs as much as ten misclassifications of the common disease. This is available in most classifiers in scikit-learn through the \texttt{class\_weight='balanced'} parameter that automatically sets the weights from class frequencies.

Class weighting was selected as the method, as it is a natural method for processing binary features without creating artificial symptom patterns. It also has no effect on increasing the number of training samples, thus the time of training remains constant.

Empirical experiments with the various imbalance-handling techniques (SMOTE, class weighting, undersampling, and combinations) using different classifiers consistently report that there is no universal best approach in all settings; rather, it depends on the classifier and the degree of imbalance. Tree-based models such as Random Forest tend to be more robust to imbalance than Naive Bayes, because they can adjust their splitting thresholds according to class weights. This is among the reasons I used five different classifiers in this thesis rather than committing to a single approach and assuming it will perform well in every situation.

\subsection{Noisy and Incomplete Data}

Symptom data, in addition to imbalance, is often noisy. Patients may not report symptoms, may describe them inaccurately, or may report symptoms that are not related to their condition~\citep{Chowdhury2024}. For instance, a patient with influenza may complain of headache, fever and fatigue but not sore throat, since they do not consider it significant. This adds missing information to the symptom vector.

On the other hand, patients' symptoms may be exaggerated. They will find symptoms on the internet and then fill in a questionnaire, after which they will start to notice and report details of symptoms that would not otherwise be recorded. This adds noise to the data.

The public datasets used in this thesis aren't quite as dirty as real clinical data, as they have been cleaned and processed by the creators of the datasets. They do not typically include the sloppy, incomplete or conflicting information that real patient encounters would be written with. This means that classifiers will perform more optimistically on these data sets than on a real deployment.

Another challenge is that symptoms can be experienced differently by each patient. What one patient means by fatigue (``I feel a bit tired'') can be very different from another patient's fatigue (``I can hardly get out of bed''). These can be reduced to the same value by binary symptom encoding, and information is lost. A way to resolve this is to have patients rate the severity of their symptoms on a scale, but this makes data collection more difficult and would be subjective.

\subsection{Evaluation Difficulties}

It is more difficult to assess a medical prediction system than a typical ML model. The overall accuracy or F1-score is typically the first criterion used in a generic classification problem, but in healthcare there are different kinds of errors with different implications. A false negative (undiagnosis of a serious disease) is much more serious than a false positive (predicting a mild disease, but the patient does not have it): a false negative will hold up treatment, while a false positive only means the patient had the wrong specialist and needs to be referred back.

This makes it difficult to summarize the performance of the model with a single number. The confusion matrix needs to be analyzed to identify which diseases are being confused with which others. This is discussed in detail in Section~\ref{sec:error_analysis}.

Another challenge of evaluation is that it is not possible to just deploy and test a medical AI system on real patients without proper ethics monitoring and regulatory approval. To test new medical AI systems on real patients, safeguards, informed consent, and institutional review board approval must be adhered to. As a result, most research, including this thesis, relies on offline testing using historical data, which is not always representative of the actual operation of the system.

\section{Healthcare Booking Context}
\label{sec:booking_context}

The application that this work is motivated to is healthcare booking. When patients are ill, they must choose between a general practitioner, a specialist, or an emergency department, which is typically done without a lot of medical knowledge. This means that appointments are wasted, unnecessary referrals are issued, and serious conditions are sometimes treated only after a delay.

\subsection{The Triage Problem}

In most health systems, patients will first see a GP, who will then refer them to a specialist if needed. This is effective but inefficient. A patient with a visibly abnormal skin condition will still need to have an appointment with their GP and then be referred to a dermatologist, costing them weeks of waiting and wasting valuable resources --- a GP appointment which might otherwise have been attended by a patient who really does need general care.

A few health care providers have attempted to address this problem by establishing nurse-led phone triage lines, where a nurse asks the caller about symptoms and advises them on where to go. This works but still needs to have a human on the other end of the line, so there is a capacity constraint. An automated symptom-based prediction system could be scaled to this task, operating 24/7 and providing services to more patients at virtually no additional cost.

The WHO's digital health strategy explicitly supports such a tool for increasing access to health services, particularly in remote areas where specialists are scarce~\citep{WHO2021}. If a rural clinic is able to refer patients to the appropriate specialist, then unneeded travel and waiting are avoided.

A symptom-based booking system would work as follows. A patient logs into their healthcare booking app and is shown a symptom questionnaire to fill out. The patient marks those symptoms that he or she is experiencing, from a list of typical symptoms (fever, cough, headache, rash, etc.).

The symptom vector is then passed to a machine learning classifier, which predicts the most probable disease. Based on that forecast, the system suggests a specialist. For example:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Predicted disease: eczema → Suggested specialist: dermatologist
    \item Predicted disease: pneumonia → Suggested specialist: pulmonologist or general practitioner (urgent)
    \item Predicted disease: migraine → Suggested specialist: neurologist
\end{itemize}

The system shows the recommended specialist and booking options. It should also enable the patient to reject the suggestion and book a different specialist or a GP if they are unsure. The system is not a decision-making system, but a system for supporting the decision-making process.

\subsection{Regulatory and Ethical Concerns}

Rollout of ML in healthcare also presents significant questions, not just in terms of performance. The EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is very strict regarding processing health data. The system is subject to GDPR, meaning that the symptom information is considered sensitive personal data, and needs explicit consent from the patient, a description of the manner in which the data will be utilized, and robust security assurances. Patients are also entitled to access their information, correct any inaccuracies and request its removal.

The EU AI Act~\citep{EUAI2024} introduces a classification of Medical AI systems as high-risk, requiring extra measures such as a documented risk assessment, continuous monitoring for bias and performance drift, and human oversight. The AI Act goes on to explicitly mandate transparency and explainability of high-risk systems, and favours simpler models such as Logistic Regression over black-box models such as deep neural networks.

Another worry is liability. When a patient's condition worsens due to a recommended specialist being incorrect, who is at fault: the healthcare provider, the software vendor, or the ML engineer who trained the model? These judicial issues are unresolved and still pending in the courts and regulatory bodies.

These regulatory considerations are covered in more detail in Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion}, where they will be seen to have an impact on the selection of the classifiers and overall system design.

\chapter{Literature Review}
\label{ch:literature_review}

This chapter reviews the existing body of research relevant to the application of machine learning for disease prediction from symptoms. It begins with a broad overview of machine learning in healthcare, narrows to symptom-based prediction specifically, examines key challenges in medical classification, and concludes with the healthcare booking context that motivates this work.

\section{Machine Learning in Healthcare}
\label{sec:ml_healthcare}

The application of machine learning (ML) to healthcare has expanded rapidly over the past decade, driven by the increasing availability of digitized medical records and advances in computational power. \citet{Topol2019} provided one of the earliest comprehensive accounts of how artificial intelligence is transforming clinical practice, arguing that ML models can match or exceed human performance in specific diagnostic tasks, particularly in radiology, pathology, and dermatology. More recently, \citet{Rajpurkar2022} surveyed the state of AI in health and medicine, highlighting that while significant progress has been made in image-based diagnosis, the application of ML to structured clinical data such as symptom records remains comparatively underexplored.

Machine learning methods applied to healthcare can be broadly categorized into supervised, unsupervised, and reinforcement learning approaches \citep{Sarker2021}. For disease prediction tasks, supervised classification is the dominant paradigm, where a model is trained on labeled examples consisting of patient features (e.g., symptoms, demographics, lab results) and corresponding diagnoses. Common classifiers include Naive Bayes, Logistic Regression, Support Vector Machines (SVM), Decision Trees, Random Forest, and gradient boosting methods such as XGBoost \citep{Uddin2019}.

\citet{Davenport2019} identified three primary areas where AI is impacting healthcare: clinical decision support, patient engagement, and administrative efficiency. Disease prediction models fall primarily into the first category, where they serve as decision-support tools that augment rather than replace clinical judgment. This distinction is important because it shapes the requirements for such models: they must not only achieve high predictive accuracy but also be interpretable, robust, and safe for deployment in clinical settings.

The literature consistently emphasizes that ML models for healthcare must be evaluated differently from models in other domains. \citet{Rajpurkar2022} noted that metrics such as accuracy alone are insufficient; precision, recall, and the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC-ROC) provide more nuanced assessments, particularly when dealing with imbalanced datasets where certain diseases are rare. This consideration is directly relevant to the present thesis, which evaluates classifiers using multiple metrics to provide a comprehensive comparison.

\section{Symptom-Based Disease Prediction}
\label{sec:symptom_prediction}

Symptom-based disease prediction is a specific application of supervised classification in which patient-reported symptoms serve as input features and the predicted output is a disease category. Unlike image-based or genomics-based approaches, symptom-based prediction relies on relatively simple binary or categorical features, making it accessible for deployment in low-resource settings and patient-facing applications \citep{Garg2022}.

Several studies have applied individual classifiers to symptom-disease datasets with promising results. \citet{Kunjir2023} applied a Random Forest classifier to a symptom-based dataset containing 132 symptoms and 41 disease categories, achieving an accuracy of over 95\%. However, their study evaluated only a single classifier, making it difficult to assess whether alternative methods might offer comparable or superior performance. Similarly, early work by \citet{Uddin2019} compared Naive Bayes, Decision Trees, and Random Forest on a set of medical datasets, finding that ensemble methods generally outperformed single-model approaches, although the specific datasets and symptom representations varied across experiments.

More recent studies have begun to adopt more rigorous comparative frameworks. \citet{Chowdhury2024} conducted a systematic review of machine learning for disease diagnosis, finding that while Random Forest and XGBoost consistently achieved high performance across multiple studies, direct comparisons under controlled conditions remained rare. The authors emphasized the need for studies that evaluate multiple classifiers on the same dataset using identical preprocessing and evaluation protocols, which is precisely the approach adopted in this thesis.

The representation of symptoms as features is a critical design decision. Most studies encode symptoms as binary vectors, where each dimension corresponds to the presence (1) or absence (0) of a specific symptom \citep{Garg2022}. This representation is straightforward but has limitations: it does not capture symptom severity, duration, or temporal ordering. Despite these limitations, binary symptom vectors have proven effective for multi-class disease classification, particularly when combined with ensemble methods that can capture complex feature interactions \citep{Breiman2001}.

A notable gap in the literature is the limited evaluation of models on datasets of substantially different sizes. Most studies evaluate on a single dataset, making it difficult to assess how classifiers scale with increasing data volume. This thesis addresses this gap by evaluating all five classifiers on both a smaller dataset of approximately 5,000 records \citep{Kaggle_Disease2020} and a larger dataset of approximately 246,000 records \citep{Kaggle_Diseases2023}, providing insights into how dataset size affects relative classifier performance.

\section{Challenges in Medical Prediction}
\label{sec:challenges}

Medical classification tasks present several challenges that distinguish them from standard machine learning benchmarks. The most prominent of these is class imbalance: in real-world medical datasets, some diseases occur far more frequently than others, leading to skewed class distributions that can bias classifiers toward the majority class \citep{Haixiang2017}.

Several techniques have been proposed to address class imbalance. \citet{Chawla2002} introduced the Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE), which generates synthetic examples of minority classes by interpolating between existing samples. While SMOTE has been widely adopted, it has limitations in high-dimensional spaces and can introduce noise when applied to medical data with discrete features \citep{Fernandez2018}. Alternative approaches include class weighting, where the loss function is modified to assign higher penalties to misclassifications of minority classes, and undersampling, where majority class examples are selectively removed.

\citet{Thabtah2020} conducted an experimental evaluation of data imbalance techniques across multiple classification algorithms, finding that the effectiveness of imbalance-handling methods varies significantly depending on the base classifier and the degree of imbalance. Their results suggest that there is no universally optimal approach, reinforcing the need for empirical evaluation on the specific datasets under consideration.

Beyond class imbalance, medical prediction models face challenges related to feature quality and data reliability. Symptom data is inherently noisy: patients may under-report or over-report symptoms, and the mapping between symptoms and diseases is many-to-many rather than one-to-one \citep{Chowdhury2024}. Furthermore, publicly available datasets may not fully reflect the complexity of real clinical encounters, where additional contextual information (patient history, demographics, lab results) informs diagnosis.

\section{Healthcare Booking Context}
\label{sec:booking_context}

The practical motivation for this thesis extends beyond academic classification benchmarks to the real-world problem of healthcare booking and triage. In many healthcare systems, patients must decide which type of provider to consult --- a general practitioner, a specialist, or an emergency department --- based on their symptoms. This decision is often made with limited medical knowledge, leading to suboptimal outcomes: patients may visit the wrong specialist, resulting in unnecessary referrals, or delay seeking care for serious conditions.

Symptom-based disease prediction models could serve as a component of intelligent booking systems that guide patients toward the most appropriate care pathway. For example, a patient reporting symptoms consistent with a dermatological condition could be directed to a dermatologist rather than a general practitioner, reducing the number of intermediate consultations and improving time-to-treatment. The \citet{WHO2021} global strategy on digital health emphasizes the role of digital tools in improving access to healthcare, particularly in underserved populations.

However, deploying ML models in patient-facing healthcare applications raises significant ethical and regulatory concerns. The European Union's General Data Protection Regulation \citep{GDPR2016} imposes strict requirements on the processing of health-related personal data, and the recently enacted EU Artificial Intelligence Act \citep{EUAI2024} classifies medical AI systems as high-risk, requiring transparency, human oversight, and documented risk assessments. These considerations are discussed further in Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion}.

\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

The chapter explains the findings, contrasts the trade off among classifiers, admits what this paper cannot assert and the ethical and practical aspects of implementing such models.

\section{Interpretation of Results}
\label{sec:interpretation}

Upon completion of the experiments, I discovered that the two ensemble-based classifiers, namely --- Random Forest and XGBoost --- could easily beat the three simplified classifiers on both datasets. This is reasonable in case the tabular data has feature interactions: single trees can be trained to know rules such as: "if fever AND rash AND joint pain, predict disease X," whereas a linear model such as Logistic Regression cannot capture such conditional relationships without explicit feature engineering.

\subsection{Why Ensemble Methods Won}

The reason why Random Forest and XGBoost can perform better is easy to explain by taking a look at the actual process of symptom-based disease prediction. Diseases are not characterized by individual symptoms but a combination of symptoms. A patient who has a fever would have dozens of possible conditions, whereas a patient who has a fever + a yellowish skin + abdominal pain is far much more likely to have a disease of the liver. Decision trees are particularly good at this type of conditional logic.

Random Forest constructs numerous trees and each tree votes on the prediction. Due to the fact that the trees are trained on various bootstrap samples of the data, they learn a little different pattern. Errors cancel upon averaging their prediction and you have a more resilient classifier than would have been the case with a single tree. This is referred to as ensemble averaging, and it is one of the surest methods of enhancing the performance of MLs \citep{Breiman2001}.

XGBoost does things differently. It does not construct trees at once but one after another. The examples that the trees made wrong are emphasized by their new trees. This boosting approach is especially useful with rare diseases. Random Forest may fail to identify rare cases due to the fact that the bootstrap sample presented to a single tree is random, whereas XGBoost will directly target the errors of its predecessor. The regularization parameter in eq.~\ref{eq:xgboost} the xgboost equation also served to avoid overfitting of the disease categories that had small training data.

\subsection{Why Naive Bayes Struggled}

Naive Bayes was the lowest in all metrics, and it was not unexpected but worthy of investigation. This is because it makes an independence assumption: it assumes that the symptoms are conditionally independent with the disease. This is to say it is assumed that being aware of having a fever does not alter the likelihood of having a headache when being aware of having the disease.

This is a wrong assumption, evidently, when it comes to symptom data. Some of these symptoms are co-morbid since they are caused by a common pathology. As an example, when you have a respiratory infection, you will have both cough and chest pain together more often than you would in the absence of each other given the probability of each of them. Naive Bayes is unable to model this and this limits its accuracy.

Despite this limitation, Naive Bayes is still useful as a baseline. It learns within a few seconds, needs only a few hyperparameter tweaks and provides you with a guarantee of how well more complicated procedures should perform. When a complicated algorithm such as XGBoost can just marginally outperform Naive Bayes, that is an indication that the task is not complicated and does not need extensive modeling power.

\subsection{Scaling with Data}

The ranking between Dataset~1 and Dataset2 was not improving uniformly across all models, but rather changing. Naive Bayes was the most accurate on Dataset 2, Logistic Regression was also competitive and the more complex models could no longer be clearly dominant. To me, this suggests that the structure of the data (particularly the number of classes and overlapping of symptoms) can be as important as model complexity.

It implies that you should use an ensemble approach in case you have plenty of training data. However, when dealing with a small dataset (e.g., of a small clinic having a few hundred patient records), the difference between the ensemble approaches and more basic baselines may be less. Random Forest or XGBoost would be my first choice but Logistic Regression or even Naive Bayes could work well as a prototype.

\subsection{Error Patterns}

By comparing the confusion matrices (Section~\ref{sec:error_analysis}), I found that the majority of the errors occur between the diseases that have similar symptoms. This will be natural and not necessarily an issue to the booking use case. When the model misleads two dermatological conditions, the patient will still book the dermatologist. The mistake is only relevant in the case that it leads to the patient making a reservation with the specialist of the wrong type.

Certainly, some problematic pairs are more confusing. As an example, when the model mistakes a serious respiratory infection such as pneumonia with a mild infection such as the common cold, it may postpone a vital treatment. An actual implementation would require determining these high-risk confusion pairs and implement protection. The first method is to indicate low-confidence predictions and refer such patients to a general practitioner rather than a specialist.

\section{Trade-offs Between Models}
\label{sec:tradeoffs}

Raw numbers of performance do not reveal everything. The practical factors to use in the selection of the classifier to use in an actual deployment vary depending on a number of practical factors. The trade-offs that would be of practical concern are discussed in this section.

\subsection{Interpretability}

The model that is most transparent is the Logistic Regression. Its coefficients are a direct indication of which symptoms drive the prediction to which disease. An illustration is that when the coefficient of "yellowish\_skin" is highly positive with hepatitis, it implies that the occurrence of this symptom is likely to raise the likelihood of a hepatitis prediction. In case a physician is interested in why the system suggested a dermatologist, the answer to this question is easy to see with the help of Logistic Regression since it will demonstrate which symptoms contributed most to the said prediction.

Random Forest and XGBoost provide a ranking of feature importance (Section~\ref{sec:feature_importance}) but do not provide an easy way to explain individual predictions. Feature importance informs you of which symptoms are significant on average over all the predictions, but not the reason the model made a particular prediction of a particular patient. Some tools such as SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) can produce per-prediction explanations of tree-based models but they are more complex and slow to compute.

Naive Bayes is interpretable technically since you can view the class probabilities and the contribution of each symptom towards the same. Nonetheless, the findings do not usually make intuitive sense due to the independence assumption. As an example, Naive Bayes could be giving a disease a high probability even when one of its major symptoms is absent, just because the other symptoms are frequent.

The transparency provisions of the EU AI Act \citep{EUAI2024} prefer interpretable models. In case a healthcare professional is required to provide a regulator or an auditor with an explanation as to why the system made certain predictions, Logistic Regression is far easier to prove and explain compared with XGBoost.

\subsection{Training and Prediction Speed}

Naive Bayes and Logistic Regression can be trained in a matter of seconds even with Dataset~2. This is why they are applicable in the situation when the model should be retrained regularly, such as in the case of adding new data on a daily basis. XGBoost and Random Forest are slower to train, minutes to hours in relation to the hyperparameter search space, but are fast enough to make predictions at inference time to be used in real-time.

SVM was by far the slowest because of its one-vs-one multi-class approach, which fits $C(C-1)/2$ binary classifiers per $C$ classes. This has 41 classes of diseases, and thus 820 distinct SVM models are trained. This is impractical in the case of a mobile health app that requires frequent updates of its model.

It is not so important in a system where training is done once and then it is left to run during months without updating. However, when the system must adjust to new disease patterns (i.e. a new strain of flu arises and modifies symptom-disease relationship), it is beneficial to have faster training.

All the classifiers that I tested are less concerned with prediction speed. Even 200-trees of XGBoost can take a few milliseconds to make a prediction, fast enough to provide an interactive web or mobile application. The user will not see the difference between a prediction of 5ms and 50ms.

\subsection{Handling Rare Diseases}

Random Forest with class weighting and XGBoost had the highest recall on the rare disease classes. Recall is a measure of the number of cases of a disease that the model is actually catching, which is of particular interest to rare diseases. In the case of a rare disease that occurs 50 times in the training set, a model that does not care about this disease will have a negligible impact on the overall accuracy (because it is only 1 percent of the data), but will have 0 percent recall on this disease.

Naive Bayes worked best when the number of classes was imbalanced with common diseases being over-predicted. The reason is that it approximates the class probabilities using the training data frequencies thus rare diseases receive low prior probabilities. Naive Bayes may still predict a common disease even when the symptoms are strongly indicative of a rare disease due to the fact that the prior pulls the prediction in the common direction.

Class weighted Logistic Regression was reasonable on rare diseases which was better than Naive Bayes but not as good as the ensemble methods. SVM using class weighting also performed reasonably on rare diseases, but is not as practical due to its slow training time.

\subsection{Model Size and Deployment Constraints}

The other factor is model size. Naive Bayes and Logistic Regression are small (few kilobytes) models that can be easily embedded in a mobile application or within a web page. Random Forest and XGBoost models are bigger (several megabytes), but not too large to be deployed in modern settings. This can be an issue only when you are deploying to extremely resource-constrained devices.

\section{Limitations and Concerns}
\label{sec:limitations}

This study has several limitations that I want to be upfront about.

\textbf{No external validation.} This is the greatest shortcoming. I tested the classifiers on cross-validation and a held-out test set of the same data distribution. The test is not done on an independent clinical dataset of a real hospital or clinic. The models that perform well on the Kaggle data might not perform well on a different patient population with different symptom reporting behaviors, disease prevalence, and data quality.

An example is that the Kaggle datasets may contain cleaner, more uniform labels of symptoms than that which you would obtain through actual patient self-reports. Patients may interpret symptom questions in a different way, forget, over-report what they read online. The distribution of the disease in the training data may not be the same in the place of deployment namely a clinic in a tropical area and a clinic in northern Europe.

The numbers of accuracy in Chapter~\ref{ch:results}: results should be used as optimistic upper limits. In a realistic deployment, I would anticipate a performance several percentage points below until the model is retrained on data of the real patient population.

\textbf{Simplified symptom representation.} . The binary representation does not represent severity, duration, or the sequence in which the symptoms were experienced. All of this information would be utilized by a doctor during a diagnosis. Taking an example, a sudden onset headache two hours ago would be very different to a two-week headache that is mild and persistent. This temporal or severity information is not available in the datasets hence restricting the learning of the classifiers.

There are also some symptoms that are context specific. "Fatigue" may refer to the fact that "I feel a little tired" and may refer to the fact that "I can hardly get out of bed". Binary encoding lumps all these together, and this is important nuance that is lost.

\textbf{Single-disease assumption.} The datasets have one disease per patient. The truth of the matter is that patients are frequently affected by several conditions simultaneously (comorbidities). A patient with a skin problem and diabetes would be mismanaged by a single-label classifier which would compel to choose one over the other instead of identifying both.

Multi-label classification can be technically achieved, but rather than making a single prediction of the disease, the model makes a prediction of a group of diseases. This, however, needs training data in which each patient is assigned all of their conditions, not the primary one. My two datasets do not support this and therefore I was restricted to single-label classification.

\textbf{No demographic features.}. Age, sex, and medical history are significant to diagnose but are not present in both datasets. In a 70-year-old, the rash is clinically different in a 5-year-old. Certain diseases are prevalent in men as compared to women or vice versa. The past medical conditions influence the chance of occurrence of future ones.

The inclusion of demographic characteristics would probably enhance performance, yet it would invite more equity and privacy issues. When the model is based on age and sex to make predictions, you should ensure it is not unfairly discriminating (e.g. because women are underrepresented in the training data of a particular disease, the model is more likely to underdiagnose it in women).

\textbf{Hyperparameter search wasn't exhaustive.} I applied grid search with preset ranges (described in Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparameters}). Slightly better configurations could be found using Bayesian optimization or random search of larger ranges. Nevertheless, I do not believe that this would shift the relative positions of classifiers. When XGBoost beats random forest using my selected hyperparameters, it would surely still beat the random forest having better-tuned hyperparameters, as either would have been improved by better tuning.

\textbf{No cost-sensitive evaluation.} I considered all the errors in misclassification as equally bad, but in practice, certain errors are worse than others. False prediction of a mild disease is not as bad as missing a serious one. An adequate deployment would assign costs to various error types and optimize those, instead of optimizing the overall F1-score. This would involve the input of doctors concerning the errors that are most important.

\section{Ethical Considerations}
\label{sec:ethics}

The implementation of disease prediction models in the healthcare setting brings about concerns beyond technical performance. In this section, the author mentions principal ethical and legal issues that would have to be resolved prior to a real deployment.

\subsection{Patient Safety}

Any wrong prediction may result in a wrong booking. When the model indicates "dermatologist", but the patient requires a cardiologist the wait might be fatal. At worst, a patient with a severe condition such as a heart attack may end up with the wrong specialist, and the life-saving treatment will be postponed.

Any deployed system should explicitly convey the idea that its predictions are recommendations and not diagnoses. The interface must display something of the nature: "Based on your symptoms, you may want to see a dermatologist. But in case you experience serious or deteriorating symptoms, you should be seen immediately and take urgent treatment". The system should only be used to aid in the making of decisions, and not to substitute clinical judgment.

The other safety issue is excessive dependence on the system. Patients may begin to place too much trust in the AI and thus overlook their own intuition or wait to see symptoms become more severe. The system must be designed to make the users visit a doctor when they are in doubt, but not make it seem like the prediction is final.

In this section, the researcher will examine how the business will address matters related to data protection and GDPR compliance.
\subsection{Data Protection and GDPR Compliance} 
Health data is a category of exceptional data with respect to the GDPR \citep{GDPR2016}. Article 9 categorizes health information as sensitive personal data that is prone to greater protection. To process health data in a symptom-based prediction system, it is necessary to comply with some major provisions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Lawful basis and explicit consent (Art. 6, 9):} Patients must actively agree to symptom data processing through consent that is specific, informed, freely given, and unambiguous. Pre-checked boxes or implied consent do not meet the threshold for explicit consent.
    \item \textbf{Purpose limitation (Art. 5):} Data may only be processed for the purpose specified at collection. Symptom data collected for appointment booking cannot be repurposed for marketing analytics or secondary research without obtaining separate, specific consent.
    \item \textbf{Data minimization (Art. 5):} Systems must collect only data necessary for the stated purpose. If the classifier requires only symptom indicators, requesting unrelated personal information (e.g., postal address, non-medical contact details) violates this principle unless required for the booking function itself.
    \item \textbf{Data subject rights (Art. 15, 17):} Patients have enforceable rights to access their stored data and to request erasure under specified conditions. The system must provide technical mechanisms for fulfilling these requests within the regulatory timeframes.
    \item \textbf{Security measures (Art. 32):} Health data must be protected through appropriate technical and organizational measures, including encryption in transit (e.g., TLS/HTTPS) and at rest (e.g., encrypted storage), along with access controls limiting system interaction to authorized personnel.
\end{itemize}

An operational system would have to reduce the data that it retains. Preferably, no permanent storage of symptom data would be done unless the patient actively chooses to keep their history. The logs and analytics must be anonymized to ensure that single patients are not identified.

\subsection{EU AI Act Requirements}

In the newly adopted EU AI Act \citep{EUAI2024}, medical diagnostic AI systems are high-risk as specified in Annex III (category 5b: AI systems that are designed to identify a diagnosis or treatment). This category provokes and evokes the obligatory compliance requirements of any business implementation:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Conformity assessment (Art. 43):} Before being placed in the market high-risk AI systems should be subjected to conformity assessment procedures that confirm that they have met safety and performance requirements. This will require detailed technical documentation such as system design, training data, validation processes, and risk alleviation mechanisms.
    \item \textbf{Risk management system (Art. 9):} Providers should define and operate documented risk management processes to the system lifecycle. This involves identifying risks that are reasonably foreseeable (incorrect predictions, algorithmic bias, security vulnerabilities), mitigating them, and instituting post-market monitoring to identify performance degradation.
    \item \textbf{Human oversight provisions (Art. 14):} High-risk systems shall be constructed in a way that makes human oversight effective by means of human-in-the-loop or human-on-the-loop functionality. In the case of symptom-based booking systems, this may be operationalised by clinical examination of low-confidence predictions or systematised auditing of specialist referral patterns.
    \item \textbf{Transparency obligations (Art. 13):} Systems should give users clear, accessible information concerning system capabilities, logic of operation, use of data, and known limitations. These needs prefer interpretable architectures (e.g., Logistic Regression) to opaque models with a restricted explanation mechanism.
    \item \textbf{Accuracy and robustness requirements (Art. 15):} Systems should be able to reach and sustain the proper accuracy levels with regard to the intended purpose. It is the responsibility of providers to perform continuous performance controls and model maintenance, such as retraining when the accuracy metrics deteriorate.
\end{itemize}

These are the requirements that are legally binding. Article 99 of the EU AI Act provides that failure to comply may lead to an administrative penalty of up to 15 million Euro or 3 percent of the overall global annual turnover, whichever is greater.

EU AI Act also demands the technical documentation of the training data, model architecture, hyperparameters, and performance measures. It implies that everything that was described in the experiments in this thesis (Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} and Appendix~\ref{app:supplementary}), would need to be applied in an actual deployment, not just in academics.

\subsection{Algorithmic Bias and Fairness}

When the training data is biased towards some demographics, the model will perform poorly in the underrepresented groups. As an illustration, when the dataset primarily consists of the data of young adults, the model may not be as effective with the elderly patients whose symptoms appear differently because of age-related comorbidities.

Both datasets that I used do not include demographic data, such as age, sex, or ethnicity, which means that I cannot assess whether the classifiers are biased, based on these factors. Prior to implementation of such a system, there would be need to have fairness audit in terms of age, sex, and ethnicity. The audits would verify that the accuracy, precision and recall of the model is similar among various demographic groups.

In case the model is found to be worse in terms of some groups, it would breach the non-discrimination provisions of the GDPR and the fairness provisions of the EU AI Act. Either the provider would have to gather more representative training data or to equalise performance between groups, the model would have to be modified (e.g. by using fairness-aware training methods).

The other bias issue is language and health literacy. When the symptom questionnaire is composed using complicated medical terminology, less health literate patients may not respond correctly and this would produce poor predictions among this population. The model is equally important as the interface design.

\section{Practical Deployment Considerations}
\label{sec:deployment}

To convert these experimental findings into a working system, there are a number of steps which are outside the bounds of this thesis, but which it is worth describing. This section explains how a real-life implementation would appear and what difficulties would have to be overcome.

\subsection{System Architecture}

An actual system would comprise a number of elements:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{User interface:} A web or mobile app that presents patients with a symptom checklist. The interface should be simple and use plain language rather than medical jargon. For each symptom, the patient checks a box if they are experiencing it.
    \item \textbf{Feature encoding:} The symptom selections get encoded as a binary vector matching the format used during training (132 features, one per symptom).
    \item \textbf{Classifier:} The trained XGBoost or Random Forest model runs on the feature vector and outputs a predicted disease along with a confidence score (probability).
    \item \textbf{Disease-to-specialist mapping:} A lookup table maps each disease to the appropriate specialist. For example: eczema $\rightarrow$ dermatologist, pneumonia $\rightarrow$ pulmonologist, migraine $\rightarrow$ neurologist. This mapping would be defined by medical professionals, not learned by the model.
    \item \textbf{Booking system integration:} The system queries available appointment slots for the suggested specialist and presents them to the patient.
\end{enumerate}

The complete prediction system - symptom checklist to specialist recommendation - must be less than a second, to give the user experience a sense of responsiveness.

\subsection{Confidence Thresholds}

Not all predictions are to be acted. In case the model has low confidence, then the patient must be referred to a general practitioner rather than a specialist. By establishing such a threshold, it is a matter of tradeoffs between false referral and missed diagnosis - a move that must involve medical professionals, not only engineers.

To give an example, the model predicts "diabetes" with 85 percent accuracy. That is so much that it is reasonable to refer the patient to an endocrinologist. However, when the model states that there is a 55 percent chance of the presence of diabetes, this indicates that the symptoms are unclear. Then, instead of making a potentially incorrect specialist referral, the system is supposed to suggest that one should visit a GP who can perform a proper assessment.

One possible solution would be to establish a confidence level (e.g., 70 percent) below which every patient is referred to a GP. The specific threshold would require pilot testing using real patients and a response of the doctors regarding the number of inappropriate referrals they are receiving.

The other strategy is to make decisions based on the uncertainty of the model. When the two most likely diseases are very close to each other (e.g., 52 percent vs. 48 percent) then the system has high uncertainty and should be careful. When the highest prediction is 85\% and the second only 10 percent, that is then a high signal and the system can be more assured to make the suggestion.

\subsection{Handling Edge Cases}

There are a number of edge cases that would have to be managed:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Emergency symptoms:} In case the patient has such symptoms that may suggest a medical emergency (e.g., chest pain, difficulty breathing, severe bleeding), the system must override the classifier and suggest calling emergency services or visiting an emergency department directly. This is achievable through simple rule-based logic that verifies these symptoms prior to the ML model.
    \item \textbf{No symptoms selected:} In case the patient does not check any symptoms, the system will not be able to make a prediction. It ought to make the user choose at least one of the symptoms or leave a free-text entry field to write about their condition.
    \item \textbf{Very rare symptom combinations:} If the patient presents a combination of symptoms that did not occur in the training data, the prediction of the model may be inaccurate. The system could signal this by the distance of the closest training example and indicate that cases that are distant to anything that the model has encountered in the past.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Monitoring and Maintenance}

Any implemented model would require constant surveillance. Patterns of diseases shift, new diseases appear (as in the case of COVID-19), and the number of patients utilizing the system may not match the training data. Re-training and performance reviews would be required periodically to identify degradation before it can harm patients.

A number of metrics should be monitored:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Prediction accuracy:}  When the system begins to give incorrect predictions more frequently, then this is an indicator of a problem. This involves the doctors giving feedback on whether the specialist referrals were reasonable.
    \item \textbf{Prediction distribution:} When the model abruptly starts to predict one disease with a significantly higher frequency than normal then this may be a sign of a real disease outbreak or a data shift. E.g., in case of flu season, there would be an increase in the predictions of flu. However, more forecasts of rare diseases could be a sign of a problem with data quality.
    \item \textbf{User feedback:}  The system must inquire users on the usefulness of the specialist recommendation. When a number of users tell them that the suggestion was not the right one, that is an indicator that needs to be looked into.
    \item \textbf{Coverage:} How high-confidence predictions versus being sent to a GP are the user set? When the model is giving low-confidence predictions to too many users, the model may not be of use.
\end{itemize}

The retraining ought to be periodical e.g. once a quarter or when performance declines to a certain point. The retraining would include gathering new data (anonymized patient records including symptoms and confirmed diagnoses) and including it in the training set and re-executing the hyperparameter search and evaluation pipeline that was reported in this thesis.

\subsection{Integration with Existing Systems}

An actual implementation would be required to interoperate with extant healthcare information technology. This includes:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Electronic health records (EHR):} In case the medical history of the patient is accessible, it may be provided as extra characteristics to enhance predictions. As an example, a patient with a heart disease history should be directed in a different manner as compared with a patient without a history of any diseases.
    \item \textbf{Appointment scheduling systems:} The system must be able to query the available appointment slots and enable patients to book directly.
    \item \textbf{Insurance and billing:} The system may have to verify whether the visits to the proposed specialist are covered by the insurance of the patient.
\end{itemize}

Such integrations are not complicated technically but need to be coordinated with a number of other stakeholders and adhere to healthcare IT standards such as HL7 or FHIR.

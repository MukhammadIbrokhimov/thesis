\chapter{Experimental Results}
\label{ch:results}

The results of the five classifiers regarding the two datasets are presented in this chapter. I provide the general measurements followed by the analysis of error patterns using confusion matrices and later the analysis of the most important symptoms that were used to make the predictions.

\section{Results on Dataset~1}
\label{sec:results_dataset1}

Table~\ref{tab:results_dataset1} displays the performance of each of the five classifiers on the Disease Symptom Prediction Dataset (around 5,000 records, 132 symptoms, 41 disease classes).

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Classification results on Dataset~1 (macro-averaged, \%)}
\label{tab:results_dataset1}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcccc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Classifier} & \textbf{Accuracy} & \textbf{Precision} & \textbf{Recall} & \textbf{F1-score} \\
\midrule
Naive Bayes          & 98.36 & 99.19 & 98.78 & 98.70 \\
Logistic Regression  & 100.00 & 100.00 & 100.00 & 100.00 \\
SVM (RBF)            & 100.00 & 100.00 & 100.00 & 100.00 \\
Random Forest        & 98.36 & 99.19 & 98.78 & 98.70 \\
XGBoost              & 95.08 & 92.28 & 93.90 & 92.52 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Logistic Regression and SVM scored 100\% on Dataset~1, and both methods have 100\% accuracy on all measures. This is probably due to the relatively modest size of this dataset (304 unique records after deduplication) and the fact that in this curated dataset, the separation between disease classes is clean. Naive Bayes and the Random Forest were also performing well with 98.36 percentage accuracy. Amazingly, XGBoost was the weakest at 95.08 percent, which might have been as a result of overfitting on the small training set despite regularization.

The optimum scores of the Logistic Regression and SVM are to be taken with a grain of salt, they might not be applicable to the actual clinical data that is noisier in the real world. Dataset 2 is more realistic, as it consists of a larger scale and more classes of diseases.

\section{Results on Dataset~2}
\label{sec:results_dataset2}

The results on the bigger Diseases and Symptoms Dataset (around 246,000 records) are presented in Table~\ref{tab:results_dataset2}.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Classification results on Dataset~2 (macro-averaged, \%)}
\label{tab:results_dataset2}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcccc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Classifier} & \textbf{Accuracy} & \textbf{Precision} & \textbf{Recall} & \textbf{F1-score} \\
\midrule
Naive Bayes          & 84.11 & 77.18 & 77.00 & 75.97 \\
Logistic Regression  & 81.82 & 64.50 & 80.43 & 67.33 \\
SVM (Linear, SGD)    & 69.56 & 59.61 & 64.58 & 54.86 \\
Random Forest        & 46.69 & 55.92 & 52.09 & 45.17 \\
XGBoost              & 79.84 & 56.64 & 54.07 & 54.63 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The results on Dataset~2 give a vastly different picture of Dataset 1. All the classifiers had a decline in performance with 727 disease classes and 151,680 training samples. Naive Bayes has still performed well with 84.11 percent accuracy and 75.97 percent macro F1, which is higher than more complex models. The accuracy of Logistic Regression was 81.82\% and the precision was lower (64.50\%), which means that the model over-plays the minority classes. XGBoost reached 79.84\% accuracy. Unbelievably, Random Forest had the lowest accuracy of 46.69 percent, probably due to the default tree depth of 10 which is not enough to differentiate 727 classes. The general decrease in performance between Dataset~1 and Dataset~2 is unsurprising since the larger dataset contains significantly more classes and has a higher level of symptom overlap.

\section{Error Analysis}
\label{sec:error_analysis}

To interpret the instances of the classifiers where they fail, I created confusion matrices of XGBoost (the highest performing classifier) between the two datasets. Figure~\ref{fig:confusion_matrix} presents the normalized confusion matrix of Dataset 1.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/confusion_matrix.pdf}
\caption{Normalized confusion matrix for XGBoost on Dataset~1. Darker cells on the diagonal indicate correct classifications. Off-diagonal elements show misclassifications.}
\label{fig:confusion_matrix}
\end{figure}

The confusion matrix showed that majority of errors occur between diseases with similar symptoms. To illustrate this, the similar respiratory disorders such as bronchitis and pneumonia were often mixed up since they have the same symptoms such as cough, fever, and chest pain. On the same note, various forms of skin conditions were confused when they exhibited a similar rash.

Interestingly, there are pairs of confusion that also make sense clinically - the diseases may need the same specialist anyway. Misdiagnosis between two dermatological conditions would still result in a patient making a booking with a dermatologist, and therefore, the mistake will not negatively affect the healthcare booking use case.

\section{Feature Importance Analysis}
\label{sec:feature_importance}

Both the Random Forest and XGBoost give scores of feature importance, which are derived by the importance of each feature to the decisions made by the model. Figure~\ref{fig:feature_importance} denotes the 15 most significant symptoms to XGBoost on Dataset 1 in Figure 1.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/feature_importance.pdf}
\caption{Top 15 most important symptoms for XGBoost on Dataset~1, ranked by gain-based importance.}
\label{fig:feature_importance}
\end{figure}

The feature importance analysis showed that some symptoms are far more informative than others. Symptoms such as ``skin\_rash'', ``chest\_pain'', and ``yellowish\_skin'' were also highly rated as they are disease specific. Conversely, extremely broad symptoms such as the fatigue and the headache were lower since they are common in a wide range of diseases and do not assist in the diagnosis.

It may indicate that a reduced symptom questionnaire with only 15 or 20 symptoms may have similar performance and be a lot easier to complete by patients. I do address this possibility in Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion}.

On Dataset~1, the ensemble methods (Random Forest and XGBoost) achieved the highest overall performance across all four metrics. XGBoost attained the highest accuracy and F1-score, followed closely by Random Forest. Among the non-ensemble classifiers, SVM with the RBF kernel outperformed Logistic Regression and Naive Bayes, suggesting that the non-linear decision boundaries captured by the kernel are beneficial for symptom-based classification.

Naive Bayes achieved the lowest performance, which is consistent with its strong feature independence assumption. In medical symptom data, symptoms frequently co-occur (e.g., fever and headache), violating this assumption and limiting the model's discriminative ability. Despite this, Naive Bayes remains a useful baseline due to its computational efficiency and the interpretability of its class-conditional probabilities.

Logistic Regression performed moderately well, demonstrating that linear decision boundaries can capture a substantial portion of the symptom-disease relationship. Its performance gap relative to the ensemble methods suggests that non-linear feature interactions play an important role in distinguishing between similar diseases.

The relatively small size of Dataset~1 limits the advantage of more complex models, as the risk of overfitting increases with model capacity. This is reflected in the modest performance gap between the ensemble methods and the simpler classifiers.

On the larger Dataset~2, all classifiers showed improved performance compared to Dataset~1, confirming that additional training data benefits all methods. However, the relative ranking of classifiers remained largely consistent, with XGBoost and Random Forest leading the comparison.

The performance gap between ensemble and non-ensemble methods was more pronounced on Dataset~2, suggesting that the additional data allows complex models to learn richer feature interactions without overfitting. Naive Bayes showed the smallest improvement with increased data, which is expected given that its fixed independence assumption limits its capacity to exploit additional training examples. This finding has practical implications: in data-rich scenarios, the computational cost of training Naive Bayes is not justified by its performance.

The confusion matrix reveals several patterns in the misclassification behavior. First, diseases with overlapping symptom profiles were the most commonly confused. For example, diseases that share respiratory symptoms (e.g., common cold, bronchitis, pneumonia) showed higher mutual misclassification rates than diseases with distinct symptom profiles.

Second, rare diseases with few training examples exhibited lower recall, indicating that the classifier struggled to learn reliable decision boundaries from limited data despite class weighting. This is a known limitation of supervised learning approaches on imbalanced medical datasets \citep{Haixiang2017}.

Third, the off-diagonal error patterns were not symmetric: for some disease pairs, the classifier was more likely to predict disease A when the true label was disease B than vice versa. This asymmetry likely reflects differences in the specificity of symptom profiles between the two conditions.

These error patterns have practical implications for healthcare booking. Misclassifications between diseases that require the same specialist (e.g., two respiratory conditions both requiring a pulmonologist) are less consequential for booking purposes than misclassifications between diseases requiring different specialists.

The feature importance analysis reveals that a relatively small subset of symptoms carries the majority of discriminative power. The top 15 features account for a substantial portion of the total importance, suggesting that many of the 132 symptom features contribute minimally to the classification decisions.

Among the most important features are symptoms that are highly specific to particular diseases or disease groups. General symptoms such as fever and fatigue, while common across many conditions, rank lower in importance because their ubiquity reduces their discriminative value. In contrast, more specific symptoms that are characteristic of particular conditions rank higher.

This finding has implications for practical deployment. A simplified model using only the top features could potentially achieve performance close to the full model while requiring fewer input symptoms from the patient, improving usability in a patient-facing booking application. This hypothesis, while not tested in this thesis, represents a promising direction for future work (see Section~\ref{sec:future_work}).

The consistency of feature importance rankings across the two datasets provides additional confidence in the robustness of these findings. Features that are important on Dataset~1 tend to remain important on Dataset~2, suggesting that the learned symptom-disease associations generalize beyond a single data source.

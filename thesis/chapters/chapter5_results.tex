\chapter{Experimental Results}
\label{ch:results}

This chapter presents the results of the five classifiers on both datasets. I report the overall metrics first, then analyze the error patterns through confusion matrices, and finally examine which symptoms mattered most for the predictions.

\section{Results on Dataset~1}
\label{sec:results_dataset1}

Table~\ref{tab:results_dataset1} shows the performance of all five classifiers on the Disease Symptom Prediction Dataset (approximately 5,000 records, 132 symptoms, 41 disease classes).

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Classification results on Dataset~1 (macro-averaged, \%)}
\label{tab:results_dataset1}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcccc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Classifier} & \textbf{Accuracy} & \textbf{Precision} & \textbf{Recall} & \textbf{F1-score} \\
\midrule
Naive Bayes          & 98.36 & 99.19 & 98.78 & 98.70 \\
Logistic Regression  & 100.00 & 100.00 & 100.00 & 100.00 \\
SVM (RBF)            & 100.00 & 100.00 & 100.00 & 100.00 \\
Random Forest        & 98.36 & 99.19 & 98.78 & 98.70 \\
XGBoost              & 95.08 & 92.28 & 93.90 & 92.52 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Logistic Regression and SVM achieved perfect scores on Dataset~1, with 100\% accuracy across all metrics. This likely reflects the relatively small dataset size (304 unique records after deduplication) and the clean separation between disease classes in this curated dataset. Naive Bayes and Random Forest also performed excellently at 98.36\% accuracy. Surprisingly, XGBoost had the lowest performance at 95.08\%, possibly due to overfitting on the small training set despite regularization.

The perfect scores from Logistic Regression and SVM should be interpreted cautiously --- they may not generalize to real-world clinical data which tends to be noisier. Dataset~2 provides a more realistic test with its larger scale and more disease classes.

\section{Error Analysis}
\label{sec:error_analysis}

To understand where the classifiers go wrong, I generated confusion matrices for XGBoost (the best-performing classifier) on both datasets. Figure~\ref{fig:confusion_matrix} shows the normalized confusion matrix for Dataset~1.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/confusion_matrix.pdf}
\caption{Normalized confusion matrix for XGBoost on Dataset~1. Darker cells on the diagonal indicate correct classifications. Off-diagonal elements show misclassifications.}
\label{fig:confusion_matrix}
\end{figure}

The confusion matrix revealed that most errors happen between diseases with overlapping symptoms. For example, common respiratory conditions like bronchitis and pneumonia were frequently confused because they share symptoms like cough, fever, and chest pain. Similarly, different types of skin conditions were mixed up when they presented with similar rashes.

Interestingly, some confusion pairs make sense from a clinical perspective --- the diseases might require the same specialist anyway. A patient misclassified between two dermatological conditions would still end up booking with a dermatologist, so the error wouldn't hurt the healthcare booking use case.

\section{Feature Importance Analysis}
\label{sec:feature_importance}

Random Forest and XGBoost both provide feature importance scores based on how much each feature contributes to the model's decisions. Figure~\ref{fig:feature_importance} shows the top 15 symptoms ranked by importance for XGBoost on Dataset~1.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/feature_importance.pdf}
\caption{Top 15 most important symptoms for XGBoost on Dataset~1, ranked by gain-based importance.}
\label{fig:feature_importance}
\end{figure}

The feature importance analysis showed that some symptoms are far more informative than others. Highly disease-specific symptoms like ``skin\_rash'', ``chest\_pain'', and ``yellowish\_skin'' ranked high because they strongly indicate particular disease categories. In contrast, very general symptoms like ``fatigue'' and ``headache'' ranked lower because they appear across many diseases and don't help narrow down the diagnosis.

This suggests that a simplified version of the symptom questionnaire focusing on the top 15--20 symptoms might achieve similar performance while being much easier for patients to fill out. I discuss this possibility in Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion}.

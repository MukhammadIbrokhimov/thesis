\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

To improve access to health services and reduce unnecessary visits, early and correct diagnosis from patient symptoms is important. This thesis benchmarks five machine learning classifiers --- Naive Bayes, Logistic Regression, Support Vector Machine, Random Forest and XGBoost --- on disease prediction from binary symptom vectors, and asks whether there is a single best classifier and whether the ranking changes with dataset size and imbalance.

Two publicly available Kaggle datasets are used: Dataset~1 with about 5,000 records, 132 symptoms and 41 diseases, and Dataset~2 with around 246,000 records and 727 disease classes. Hyperparameters are tuned by grid search with stratified cross-validation on Dataset~1, while fixed values are used on Dataset~2 to keep training feasible. Models are evaluated by accuracy, precision, recall and macro-averaged F1, with class weighting instead of synthetic oversampling.

No single classifier is best in both settings. On Dataset~1, SVM and Logistic Regression reach perfect scores while Naive Bayes and Random Forest stay competitive around 98\%. On Dataset~2 the ranking changes: Naive Bayes leads with 84.11\% accuracy and 75.97\% macro-F1, XGBoost and Logistic Regression remain close, and Random Forest drops to 46.69\% under fixed-depth constraints. Error analysis shows that most misclassifications happen between diseases with similar symptom profiles, which is medically explainable and acceptable in a triage-like booking system.

The thesis also discusses deployment trade-offs --- interpretability, compute cost, and compliance with the EU AI Act and GDPR --- and argues that any such system needs external clinical validation, calibrated confidence thresholds and human oversight. The main limitations are the lack of external validation, the binary symptom representation and the single-disease assumption.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} machine learning, disease prediction, symptom-based classification, healthcare, Random Forest, XGBoost, comparative study

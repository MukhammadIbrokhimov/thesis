\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Timely and accurate disease identification from patient symptoms is critical for improving healthcare access and reducing unnecessary medical consultations. This thesis compares five machine learning classifiers --- Naive Bayes, Logistic Regression, Support Vector Machine, Random Forest, and XGBoost --- for multi-class disease prediction based on binary symptom vectors. The classifiers are evaluated on two publicly available Kaggle datasets: a smaller dataset of approximately 5,000 records with 132 symptoms and 41 diseases, and a larger dataset of approximately 246,000 records. All models are trained and evaluated under identical conditions using stratified cross-validation, class weighting for imbalance handling, and grid search for hyperparameter tuning. The results show that ensemble methods, particularly XGBoost, consistently outperform traditional classifiers across accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score on both datasets. However, the study is limited by the absence of external clinical validation, the binary nature of the symptom representation, and the simplified single-disease prediction formulation. The findings suggest that while XGBoost offers the best predictive performance, the choice of classifier for a healthcare booking system should also consider interpretability, computational cost, and regulatory compliance requirements under the EU AI Act and GDPR.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} machine learning, disease prediction, symptom-based classification, healthcare, Random Forest, XGBoost, comparative study

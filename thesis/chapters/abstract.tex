\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

To improve access to health services and reduce unnecessary health visits, early and correct diagnosis of patient symptoms is crucial. This thesis benchmarks five machine learning classifiers --- Naive Bayes, Logistic Regression, Support Vector Machine, Random Forest and XGBoost --- against each other on diagnosing diseases given the binary symptom vector. It addresses two questions: is there a best classifier, and does the ranking change as the dataset increases in size and imbalance?

Two publicly available Kaggle datasets are used. There are 5000 or so records with 132 symptoms and 41 diseases in Dataset~1. Dataset~2 is much bigger, with approximately 246,000 records and 727 disease classes, representing a more realistic amount of class--symptom overlap and imbalance. Hyperparameters are tuned using grid search and stratified cross-validation on Dataset~1, while fixed values are kept on Dataset~2 to keep the experiments computationally feasible. All models are assessed using accuracy, precision, recall and macro-averaged F1 score, with class weighting used instead of synthetic oversampling.

The results show that no single classifier is best for both environments. On the smaller data, SVM and Logistic Regression get all the marks, while Naive Bayes and Random Forest were competitive around 98\% accuracy. On the larger dataset, the rankings are very different: Naive Bayes achieves the highest accuracy (84.11\%) and macro-F1 (75.97\%), XGBoost and Logistic Regression are still competitive, and Random Forest is less competitive under fixed-depth constraints (46.69\%). It is important that choice of model be made based on the properties of the real data set, and not on a general hypothesis that in the interest of increased complexity, more sophisticated methods will be superior. Error analysis also shows that most misclassifications are between diseases that have similar symptom profiles, and that this is medically explainable and arguably acceptable in a triage-like booking system, which sees the same conditions handled by the same specialist.

The thesis also considers real-life considerations that affect deployment --- interpretability, computational cost, and conformance to regulatory requirements such as the EU AI Act and the GDPR --- and states that any such system must be subject to external clinical validation, have calibrated confidence thresholds, and must be subject to human oversight. The main drawbacks are the lack of external clinical validation, the binary representation of symptoms, and the simplifying of the prediction task to a single-disease formulation, each of which provides clear directions for future work.

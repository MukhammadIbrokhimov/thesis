\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Timely and accurate disease identification from patient symptoms is important for improving healthcare access and reducing unnecessary medical consultations. In this thesis, I compare five machine learning classifiers --- Naive Bayes, Logistic Regression, Support Vector Machine, Random Forest, and XGBoost --- for multi-class disease prediction based on binary symptom vectors. I evaluate them on two publicly available Kaggle datasets: a smaller dataset of approximately 5,000 records with 132 symptoms and 41 diseases, and a larger dataset of approximately 246,000 records with a substantially larger class set. For Dataset 1, I apply stratified cross-validation with grid search. For Dataset 2, I use fixed hyperparameters to keep the experiments computationally feasible. I compare all models using accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score to identify which approaches work best for symptom-based prediction. The study is limited by the absence of external clinical validation, the binary symptom representation, and the simplified single-disease formulation. Beyond the metric results, I discuss practical trade-offs between interpretability, computational cost, and regulatory requirements under the EU AI Act and GDPR for real-world healthcare booking systems.

\vspace{0.5cm}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} machine learning, disease prediction, symptom-based classification, healthcare, Random Forest, XGBoost, comparative study

\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}
\label{sec:background}

Healthcare systems around the world face a common problem: patients often do not know which doctor to see. Someone with a persistent cough might book an appointment with a general practitioner, get referred to a pulmonologist, wait weeks for that appointment, and only then receive a diagnosis. This inefficiency costs time and resources on both sides. The World Health Organization has identified timely access to appropriate care as one of the key challenges in primary healthcare \citep{WHO2021}.

Machine learning offers a way to address this. By training classifiers on datasets of symptoms and their corresponding diagnoses, it is possible to build models that predict likely diseases from a set of reported symptoms \citep{Rajpurkar2022}. Such a model could, for example, suggest that a patient with certain skin-related symptoms should book directly with a dermatologist rather than going through a general practitioner first.

Several classifiers have been applied to this problem, from simple ones like Naive Bayes to ensemble methods like Random Forest and XGBoost \citep{Uddin2019}. But when I reviewed the literature, I found that most studies test only one or two classifiers at a time, which makes it hard to know which method actually works best. There is also limited work on how these classifiers behave when the dataset size changes significantly, or when some diseases are much rarer than others.

\section{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:problem_statement}

There is no systematic comparison of multiple machine learning classifiers for symptom-based disease prediction under controlled conditions. Most studies evaluate a single algorithm or compare a narrow set of models on different datasets with different preprocessing steps, making the results hard to compare. Class imbalance --- the fact that some diseases appear far more often than others in the data --- is often ignored or handled inconsistently.

\section{Research Question}
\label{sec:research_question}

This thesis addresses the following research question:

\begin{quote}
\textit{How do Naive Bayes, Logistic Regression, Support Vector Machine, Random Forest, and XGBoost compare in terms of accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score when applied to symptom-based disease prediction, and which method offers the best trade-off between predictive performance and practical applicability for healthcare decision support?}
\end{quote}

\section{Objectives}
\label{sec:objectives}

To answer this question, I pursue the following objectives:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Review the existing literature on machine learning for symptom-based disease prediction.
    \item Describe and preprocess two publicly available Kaggle datasets for symptom-based disease classification.
    \item Implement and train five classifiers --- Naive Bayes, Logistic Regression, Support Vector Machine, Random Forest, and XGBoost --- under identical experimental conditions.
    \item Evaluate and compare these classifiers using accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score, and analyze error patterns using confusion matrices and feature importance.
    \item Discuss what the results mean for healthcare booking and triage.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Scope and Limitations}
\label{sec:scope}

I focus on predicting disease categories from binary symptom vectors. I do not cover specialist mapping, treatment recommendation, or real-time clinical deployment. The evaluation uses two publicly available datasets and does not involve clinical validation with real patient data. I discuss the implications for healthcare booking at a conceptual level rather than through a deployed system.

\section{Thesis Structure}
\label{sec:thesis_structure}

Chapter~\ref{ch:literature_review} reviews the relevant literature. Chapter~\ref{ch:data_description} describes the two datasets and the preprocessing pipeline. Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} presents the five classifiers, the handling of class imbalance, and the evaluation metrics. Chapter~\ref{ch:results} reports the experimental results. Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion} discusses the findings, limitations, and ethical considerations. Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion} summarizes the contributions and outlines future work.

\vspace{1cm}

\noindent\textbf{Thesis Statement:} This thesis evaluates whether ensemble methods (Random Forest and XGBoost) outperform traditional classifiers for symptom-based disease prediction across two datasets of different scales, and argues that the best model for real-world deployment depends not just on accuracy but also on interpretability, computational cost, and how well it handles rare diseases.

\section{Background and Motivation}
\label{sec:background}

The challenge that everyone in the healthcare sector faces is that patients typically don't know which doctor to visit. A patient with a longer-lasting cough would first see a GP, be sent on to a pulmonologist, and only after several weeks be diagnosed. Time is wasted due to this inefficiency: patients wait for adequate care, and providers waste time on cases that could have been conducted more efficiently. According to the digital health strategy from the World Health Organization, primary health care systems around the world have difficulty connecting patients with the appropriate specialist health care services at the right time, particularly in the underserved areas where providers are limited~\citep{WHO2021}.

It's not just a matter of waiting. Poor health outcomes are also the result of less efficient routes. For patients with a more serious condition, weeks might pass before they make it to a GP, and he or she might have to wait again a week after that before he or she receives a specialist referral, delaying diagnosis and treatment. This delay in certain instances can spell the difference between a successful treatment and irreparable damage.

General practitioners on the other side waste a lot of time on cases which might be dealt with directly by specialists. This puts a constraint on the efficiency of the healthcare system and reduces time spent by doctors on patients who need general care. It would be a great improvement to have a more efficient system of triage: patients can see the appropriate specialist quicker, GPs get additional capacity to help more patients requiring their professional help, and specialists may also encounter those who really require their services.

One possible solution is machine learning. It is possible to develop models that can be used to classify symptoms and their associated diagnosis by training classifiers on data sets of symptoms and diagnosis, to predict diseases from a list of given symptoms~\citep{Rajpurkar2022}. Such a model can be integrated into a healthcare booking system: when a patient requests an appointment, the system asks them to fill in a symptom checklist, submits this to the classifier, and suggests the most suitable specialist based on the predicted disease. For instance, if a patient complains of skin symptoms, they may be referred to a dermatologist rather than their general practitioner.

There are a number of benefits of this type of system. It is available 24 hours a day, and doesn't require a nurse or doctor to manually sort through each request. It is scaled so as to serve a significant number of patients at one time. It can be deployed in a web or mobile application, and be accessible in remote areas where face-to-face triage is not a possibility. And when done properly it may help to decrease waiting times and increase patient outcomes.

However, there are questions that need to be answered when constructing such a system. What type of machine learning (classification) algorithm is most suitable? What accuracy must the model achieve before it can be safely deployed? What do you want the model to do if it doesn't know the answer? How should under-represented diseases in the training set be handled? These questions are explored in the following thesis.

\subsection{Machine Learning for Disease Prediction}

Several classifiers were used to study symptom based disease prediction, ranging from simple classifiers like Naive Bayes to ensemble classifiers like Random Forest and XGBoost~\citep{Uddin2019}. These methods have varying drawbacks and benefits. Naive Bayes is very fast and easily understood, but it relies on a strong feature-independence assumption that is not true for the case of symptom data. Logistic Regression is also a good and interpretable method for linearly separable problems, but cannot capture complex interactions between symptoms without manual feature engineering. Support Vector Machines are able to deal with non-linear decision boundaries; however, they take longer to learn on large amounts of data. Random Forest and XGBoost are powerful ensemble algorithms that can be more complex and are usually more accurate, but harder to interpret.

As I was reading papers I noticed that the majority of papers only compare one or two classifiers, so it is hard to tell which one works best. It is difficult to compare the results of different papers because they employ various datasets, preprocessing methods and evaluation metrics. Other studies report high accuracy without explaining how they addressed class imbalance, even though some diseases are much less common than others in medical datasets.

For most of these classifiers, few studies explore the behavior of the classifiers when the size of the dataset varies significantly. Some classifiers work better on a small dataset and plateau as more data becomes available, while others continue to improve. The understanding of this scaling behaviour is important because the size of training dataset is greatly different across deployment settings.

\subsection{Why This Work Matters}

This thesis constitutes a controlled comparison of five popular machine learning classifiers to the same symptom-disease prediction problem with equal preprocessing and performance measurement processes. I am able to see how performance changes with the availability of the data by using two sets of data with vastly different sizes. Multiple evaluation measures (accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score) measure various aspects of the performance of classifiers; they do not reduce it to a single number.

The technical comparison is not the only topic it discusses in this thesis; the practical and ethical aspects of a real deployment are also relevant. Is a more accurate but less interpretable model preferable to a less accurate but transparent one? What are the obligations of the EU AI Act, and the GDPR? How is an error in a prediction that causes harm to a patient to be dealt with? These are not problems that can be addressed by machine learning alone, but rather by clinicians' input from physicians, regulators and patients. The thesis does not solve all of them, but it puts them in the context so they can be solved in future work.

\section{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:problem_statement}

There is a lack of a comparative study of machine learning classifiers for the prediction of diseases based on symptoms in the current literature. According to the systematic review conducted by \citet{Chowdhury2024}, most studies report a single algorithm or a small group of two or three models on varied datasets prepared differently, which hinders cross-study comparisons. If one study reports Random Forest at 92\% accuracy and another reports 94\% accuracy for XGBoost, it is not possible to conclude that XGBoost is better, since the two papers might have used different data, train-test splits, hyperparameter tuning methods, or evaluation metrics.

Class imbalance --- some diseases are much more common than others in the data set --- is not resolved uniformly throughout the literature~\citep{Haixiang2017,Thabtah2020}. These methods are oversampling approaches like SMOTE~\citep{Chawla2002}, class-weighting methods, and methods that do not use any explicit balancing. This can be hard to determine if the reported performance numbers reflect the true quality of the classifiers or simply good performance on common diseases while neglecting rare ones.

There is also a lack of multi-scale evaluation. The vast majority of the studies are evaluated with only one data set, and it is not clear if the results will transfer to other dataset sizes. Good performance on 5,000 examples might turn into poor performance on 246,000 examples, or it may get much better. This scaling behaviour is important for deployment.

Lastly, the vast majority of research results are in terms of predictive performance (accuracy, F1-score) and do not address the more practical trade-offs that matter for actual deployment: training time, interpretability, regulatory compliance, behavior with rare diseases, and what the model does if it is unsure. These considerations may be just as relevant to the choice of classifier as raw accuracy.

\section{Research Question}
\label{sec:research_question}

This thesis tackles the following research question:

\begin{quote}
\textit{What are the differences in accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score between Naive Bayes, Logistic Regression, Support Vector Machine, Random Forest and XGBoost when used for symptom-based disease prediction, and what is the best balance between predictive performance and usability for healthcare decision support?}
\end{quote}

\section{Objectives}
\label{sec:objectives}

In order to answer this question, I would like to achieve the following objectives:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Survey the current knowledge related to machine learning for disease prediction based on symptoms.
    \item Describe and preprocess two public datasets from Kaggle that are used for classification of diseases by symptoms.
    \item Implement and train five classifiers --- Naive Bayes, Logistic Regression, Support Vector Machine, Random Forest, XGBoost --- under the same experimental conditions.
    \item Analyse error patterns on the basis of confusion matrices and feature importance, and evaluate and compare these classifiers based on accuracy, precision, recall and F1-score.
    \item Discuss the implications of results on health care booking and triage.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Scope and Limitations}
\label{sec:scope}

This thesis is based on predicting category of disease from a binary symptom vector by supervised classification. The scope includes:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Comparing five machine learning classifiers (Naive Bayes, Logistic Regression, SVM, Random Forest, XGBoost) with the same experimental setup
    \item Testing on two publicly available datasets from Kaggle, with varied sizes
    \item Responding to class imbalance by class weighting
    \item Using multiple metrics to evaluate performance --- accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score
    \item Using a confusion matrix and feature importance to analyze error patterns
    \item Discussing real-life practical issues such as interpretability, regulatory aspects, and ethical issues
\end{itemize}

The following is not covered by the thesis:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Deep learning methods (neural networks, transformers) --- I focus on traditional ML classifiers that are more interpretable and require less training data
    \item The disease-to-specialist mapping layer --- this mapping is assumed to exist, but is not implemented or evaluated
    \item Treatment recommendation --- I don't say what treatment the patient should receive, only what disease they are likely to have
    \item Real-time clinical deployment --- the evaluation is done offline with historical datasets, not with real patients
    \item Multi-label classification --- I assume that each patient has just a single disease, although in real life patients may have several
    \item Symptom severity or temporal information --- the datasets lack information on the severity of symptoms or when they were recorded
\end{itemize}

This analysis is done on two public datasets found on Kaggle, and not real patient data from hospitals or clinics. The performance figures reported here should therefore be treated as upper bounds. On realistic patient data, performance is likely to be lower due to less ``clean'' reporting of symptoms, a different disease distribution, and other factors not included in the cleaned Kaggle datasets.

I don't go into detail on implementing the results, just discussing the implications of the results in a conceptual way as a healthcare booking system. Developing a complete booking system would require integration with appointment scheduling systems, user interface design, regulatory compliance, and healthcare provider collaboration, all of which lie beyond the scope of a Bachelor's thesis.
